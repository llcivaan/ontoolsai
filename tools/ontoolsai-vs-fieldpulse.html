What makes FieldPulse different from Jobber and Housecall Pro

FieldPulse occupies a specific position in the field service software market that is worth understanding before comparing it to anything else. It is not trying to be the simplest or cheapest option. It is built for trade businesses that have specific operational complexity Jobber cannot handle.

The three things FieldPulse genuinely does better than Jobber: custom job workflow stages, per-property asset tracking, and multi-day project management. If your jobs fit the standard "schedule → complete → invoice" pattern that most residential service calls follow, Jobber covers this at lower cost. If your jobs involve multiple visits over multiple days, require tracking specific equipment at specific customer sites (boilers, HVAC units, electrical panels), or need workflow stages that match your actual trade process (permit pulled → materials ordered → inspection scheduled), FieldPulse gives you control that Jobber's more rigid system does not.

FieldPulse raised $50 million in Series C funding in August 2025 and has been expanding into AI features — Operator AI (a 24/7 voice receptionist that handles after-hours calls) and Chat AI (a website chatbot for lead capture). These are meaningful additions for trade businesses with high inbound call volume. The honest assessment from 2026 reviews is that these AI features work for basic call handling and are improving, but still require human backup for complex scenarios and add to an already non-transparent pricing structure.

The pricing transparency gap

FieldPulse's opaque pricing adds genuine friction when you are comparing FSM platforms. Jobber publishes four clear tiers from $69 to $349 per month. Housecall Pro publishes three tiers from $79 per month. FieldPulse requires you to start a trial or speak to sales before knowing what you will pay. This is a deliberate strategy — it allows FieldPulse to price based on your team size and negotiating position rather than competing on published numbers. If pricing transparency matters to you, factor in the time cost of a sales conversation before committing to evaluate FieldPulse against its competitors.

How FieldPulse performs across trades — an honest assessment

HVAC (7/10): FieldPulse handles HVAC service call scheduling and invoicing adequately. The Operator AI is genuinely useful for after-hours emergency calls — a broken AC or heating failure at 10pm is exactly the scenario where an AI voice receptionist that can answer the call and book a job adds real value. The gap: no equipment tracking per installation site at the depth most commercial HVAC businesses need, no automated maintenance reminder scheduling, and no integration with HVAC-specific tools like flat-rate pricebooks. For residential HVAC doing mostly service calls, it is adequate. For commercial HVAC managing preventive maintenance contracts across multiple sites, the limitations show.

Plumbing (6.5/10): Works well for straightforward plumbing service calls. The custom job workflow stages help with jobs that have regulatory steps — permit-required work where you need to track "permit submitted," "permit approved," and "inspection booked" as distinct stages. The limitation is that FieldPulse's asset tracking is less granular than plumbing-specific tools for tracking pipe configurations, warranty status, and equipment history per property.

Electrical (7.5/10): FieldPulse is a stronger fit for electrical businesses than many general FSM platforms because the custom workflow stages align well with how electrical jobs actually move — site survey, quote, permits, installation phases, sign-off, invoice. For residential electrical doing standard service work this is overkill, but for commercial electrical with multi-day installations, the project management structure earns its cost.

Landscaping (7/10): The multi-day project management and custom workflow stages work well for landscaping businesses doing large design-and-build projects. For recurring weekly maintenance contracts, the complexity FieldPulse adds over Jobber is not worth the cost difference. The right tool for a landscaping business depends heavily on whether your revenue comes primarily from project work or recurring maintenance.

Cleaning (5/10): FieldPulse is not a natural fit for cleaning businesses. The features that justify its premium over Jobber — multi-day project management, per-property asset tracking, custom technical workflow stages — are largely irrelevant for a business whose jobs are recurring cleans at residential or commercial properties. ZenMaid or Jobber are better fits for cleaning at lower cost.

The communication gap FieldPulse leaves open

FieldPulse is significantly more sophisticated than most FSM platforms in how it handles operational workflows. What it shares with every other FSM platform is the same communication gap — the moment where automated templates end and judgment begins.

FieldPulse sends automated operational messages well: appointment confirmations, job status updates, invoice notifications. Its Operator AI can even handle after-hours calls and book jobs without a human. These are real and useful capabilities that reduce admin burden for trade businesses with high inbound volume.

The gap opens in three specific situations that drive a significant portion of revenue outcomes. First: the quote that went cold. A technician visits a commercial site for an electrical assessment, produces a detailed quote, and three days later the silence begins. FieldPulse has no mechanism for the follow-up message that gives the customer a specific reason to respond — the message that addresses their likely objections (price, timing, comparing options), offers a specific next step, and makes it easy to say no without the relationship becoming awkward. This message, sent consistently on Day 3 and Day 10 across every unanswered quote, converts a meaningful percentage of leads that would otherwise be written off.

Second: the review request. After completing a complex multi-day electrical installation, the customer is pleased. The relief of having the work done cleanly and on schedule is at its peak. This is the exact window — typically within 2 hours of the final sign-off — where a personalised review request from the business owner converts at the highest rate. FieldPulse can trigger an automated reminder, but a message that references the specific project, uses the customer's name, and comes across as a genuine personal ask from the person who ran the job performs meaningfully differently from a platform-generated notification.

Third: the complaint that arrives after a multi-day job. Something did not work as expected. The customer is frustrated. The message that de-escalates this situation and preserves the relationship — while potentially protecting against a 1-star review — requires specific judgment about what happened, who the customer is, and what an appropriate remedy looks like. No automation engine handles this. OnToolsAI writes it in 30 seconds based on the specific situation.

Who is FieldPulse best suited for?

No. FieldPulse sends automated operational notifications — appointment confirmations, job status updates, basic payment reminders. It does not write personalised customer messages. The quote follow-up that converts a hesitant customer three days after a site visit, the review request sent within 2 hours of completing a job, the invoice reminder that preserves a long-term client relationship — these require OnToolsAI.
Is FieldPulse better than Jobber?
It depends on your business stage and job complexity. Jobber is simpler, cheaper, and better for basic residential service work. FieldPulse is more powerful for businesses doing multi-day installations, commercial service contracts, or complex jobs that need custom workflow stages and per-property asset tracking. For a 2 to 4 person residential team doing mostly service calls, Jobber is likely the better starting point. For a 6 to 15 technician business doing complex installations alongside service work, FieldPulse earns its premium.
