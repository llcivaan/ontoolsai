1. The seasonal communication calendar — when to send what

The single most valuable thing a landscaping business can do is plan its communication calendar around the season — not reactively, but proactively. The businesses that fill their diary first every spring are the ones that started communicating in January.

🌱 Spring (Mar–May) — Peak booking
Quote follow-ups urgently. Promo posts for spring packages. Review requests after first jobs. Social media peaks here — use it. Seasonal reactivation emails to past clients who haven't yet booked.
☀️ Summer (Jun–Aug) — Full execution
Running-late messages when schedules slip. Review requests after big projects. Invoice chasing on outstanding balances. Referral asks from happy clients. Hire seasonal staff.
🍂 Autumn (Sep–Nov) — Wind-down + upsell
Leaf clearance and winter prep upsell to existing clients. End-of-season review requests. Chase outstanding invoices before year-end. Start thinking about next year's contracts.
❄️ Winter (Dec–Feb) — Reactivation season
Reactivation campaign to past clients. Early-bird spring booking promos. Quote follow-up on any outstanding proposals. Plan your spring communication calendar.
💡 The January email. A landscaping business that sends a warm reactivation message to all past clients in January — "Just reaching out before spring to see if you'd like to get on the books again" — consistently fills its April and May diary before competitors even start marketing. The clients who book in January came back because you reached out first.

2. Quote follow-ups — the spring booking rush

Spring is when landscaping quotes convert best — and also when they go quiet fastest, because every landscaper in the area is quoting the same surge of enquiries. Homeowners get two or three quotes and then delay while they think about it. Your follow-up needs to add two things that the other roofers probably aren't adding: specificity about what you observed at their property, and genuine urgency about spring availability.

A message that references something specific from your site visit — "the soil near the north fence is going to need a bit of work before those beds are ready" — immediately differentiates you from a competitor who sent a generic quote. It signals expertise and genuine engagement with their specific garden.

3. Review strategy — the end-of-season window

Landscaping reviews convert best at two moments: just after a significant project completion (new patio, full garden redesign, major planting), and at the end of the season when a client can see the full picture of what their garden looked like under your care all year.

The post-project window is narrow — 24-48 hours after completion, while the transformation is fresh and the client hasn't yet moved past the excitement of a new garden. The end-of-season window is more forgiving but equally powerful: "You've had the garden looking brilliant this year — if you had a moment to leave a review, it would mean a lot to us."

4. Complaint handling — visible work, high expectations

Landscaping complaints are unique because the work is highly visible and the client has strong opinions about what their garden should look like. A complaint about a hedge that was cut too aggressively, plants that didn't survive, or a lawn that looks worse after treatment can feel very personal — the garden is an extension of the home.

Respond quickly, take it seriously, and offer to come back and assess. The temptation to explain why the hedge needed that cut, or why the plant died, is one to resist in the first message. Acknowledge first. Explain second — in person if possible, once trust is re-established.

5. Reactivating past clients in the off-season

Every landscaping business has a list of past clients who used them once or twice and then went quiet. Some went with a cheaper option. Some moved. Some just got busy and forgot to rebook. But a meaningful percentage of them are waiting to be asked — they liked you, they just didn't have a reason to reach out.

The winter reactivation campaign is one of the highest-ROI communication moves in landscaping. A warm message in January or February — referencing previous work, offering an early-bird booking incentive, and making it easy to respond — consistently converts 15-25% of past clients into spring bookings.

6. Invoice and payment communication

Landscaping payment terms vary widely — some businesses invoice per visit, others monthly, others at project completion. Whatever your terms, chase promptly and professionally. End-of-season invoices are particularly important to chase before clients mentally close out the year.

7. Hiring for seasonal demand

Seasonal hiring is one of the hardest things in landscaping. You need more people in spring, fewer in winter, and you need the good ones to come back the following year. The businesses that rehire their best seasonal workers every spring are the ones who communicated well during the off-season — a brief message in autumn saying "we'd love to have you back next year if you're interested" costs almost nothing and solves the recruitment problem before it starts.
