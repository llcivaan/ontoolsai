Plumbing Communication Guide

Plumbing has a communication challenge that most trades don't: you're dealing with two completely different customer types in the same business. Emergency customers are stressed, making decisions under pressure, and often have no idea what things cost. Planned-work customers are relaxed, comparing quotes, and weighing up options. Every message type in your business needs to work for both.

1. Emergency vs planned — two different communication modes

The customer who calls you at 8pm because their bathroom is flooding is in a fundamentally different emotional state from the one who books a boiler service three weeks in advance. Your communication — before, during, and after the job — needs to reflect that difference.

🚨 Emergency customers
Stressed, no frame of reference for cost, relief-driven after the job. Fast responses are everything. Post-job communication has a narrow window. Reviews convert best in the first 2 hours.
📋 Planned work customers
Comparing 2-3 quotes, price-sensitive, time-flexible. Follow-up sequences work. Reviews should be requested same-day but less urgently. Relationship communication matters more.

The practical implication: keep two mental templates for every message type. "Running late" to an emergency customer needs to include a practical instruction for what to do while they wait. "Running late" to a boiler service booking is a lighter-touch apology. The same principle applies to complaints, review requests, and quote follow-ups.

2. Quote follow-ups that convert without pressure

Most plumbing quote follow-ups fail because they're written as if the customer forgot about the quote. They didn't forget — they're weighing it up, comparing others, or waiting to see if the problem gets worse before committing to the repair. A follow-up that helps them make a decision converts far better than one that just nudges them to respond.

For larger jobs (bathroom fitting, pipe relining, significant repairs), a 3-message sequence over three weeks is optimal. For smaller jobs, one well-timed follow-up at 4-5 days is usually enough. The key is always to add something useful — a seasonal note, a practical observation from your site visit, or an honest answer to the question you know they're probably asking.

3. Running late — why it hits harder for plumbers

Being 30 minutes late as a plumber is categorically different from being late in almost any other trade. Your customer may have their main stopcock turned off, no water in the house, or water actively spreading through a ceiling. They're not patiently waiting — they're managing a situation.

The running-late message for a plumber needs to do something that messages in other trades don't: it needs to give the customer a practical instruction for the wait. One sentence — "if you haven't already, keep the isolation valve closed" or "you don't need to do anything, just leave it as it is" — transforms an anxious wait into a managed one. That single sentence is also the reason plumbers get reviews that mention "professional and knowledgeable."

4. Review strategy — the post-emergency window

Plumbers have a natural advantage in reviews that most trades don't: you end emergency jobs with a deeply relieved customer. A burst pipe fixed, hot water restored, a flooding bathroom brought back under control. The relief in those moments is the most powerful driver of a 5-star review you will ever have. The window for asking is narrow — a few hours — and most plumbers miss it entirely by asking too late or not at all.

The rule: ask on the day, always. For emergencies, on your way back from the property. For planned service jobs, that evening at the latest. After 24 hours, the relief has faded, the customer has moved on, and the motivation to write a review is a fraction of what it was in the immediate aftermath of a solved problem.

5. Complaint and bad review handling

The majority of plumbing complaints fall into two categories: quality (it's still leaking) and price (it cost more than expected). Both require an empathetic first response that opens a conversation rather than defending a position. The temptation to explain, justify, or correct is strongest exactly when it's most harmful — in the first message, before you've re-established any trust.

For quality complaints, offer a return visit immediately and without qualification. For pricing complaints, offer to walk through the invoice together. Never reduce a bill just to avoid a conversation — that trains every future customer to challenge your pricing.

6. Invoice and payment communication

Plumbing invoices have a specific payment challenge: the customer agreed to the rate under pressure and now the stress has passed. The invoice can feel disproportionate to a situation that no longer feels urgent. This is why plumbing has higher invoice dispute rates than most trades — and why the first reminder needs to be handled with particular care.

A warm, human first reminder that assumes oversight (not bad faith) resolves the majority of overdue accounts immediately. Escalate tone progressively across three messages. Document every contact attempt from the start — it matters if you end up in small claims.

💡 Ask for payment upfront on larger jobs. A 30-50% deposit on bathroom fits, pipe relining, and major repairs is standard practice and increasingly expected. Customers who pay a deposit are far less likely to dispute the final invoice — they've already invested in the outcome.

7. Hiring plumbers in a tight market

Skilled plumbers have genuine options. A job ad that lists certification requirements and a vague salary range won't reach the candidates you want — they're already employed and only looking if something catches their eye. The businesses that hire the best plumbers write ads that describe what working there actually feels like: the kit, the autonomy, the types of jobs, the owner who knows the trade.
